# Justiça Eleitoral

Structure and jurisdiction of the specialized electoral court system
(TSE, TREs, juízes eleitorais, zonas eleitorais). This file covers
the *institutional* layer — composition, tenure, competência, and the
territorial unit (zona eleitoral) that anchors electoral jurisdiction.

## Constitutional basis

`CF.118`–`CF.121` establish the Justiça Eleitoral as a specialized
branch of the federal judiciary. Organs: TSE, TREs (one per state +
DF), juízes eleitorais, juntas eleitorais. Detailed competência and
procedure are delegated to the Código Eleitoral (Lei 4.737/1965) and
to LOMAN (LC 35/1979).

## TSE — Tribunal Superior Eleitoral

**Composition** (`CF.119`): 7 members — 3 ministros do STF (elected
by secret vote among STF peers), 2 ministros do STJ (elected by
secret vote among STJ peers), 2 advogados chosen by the President
from a list of 6 prepared by the STF.

**Tenure** (`CF.121`): 2-year term, maximum two consecutive terms.
Members enjoy *inamovibilidade* during the term.

**President and vice-president**: elected among the STF ministers on
the court. The corregedor-geral eleitoral is chosen among the STJ
ministers.

## TRE — Tribunais Regionais Eleitorais

**Composition** (`CF.120`): 7 members per TRE — 2 desembargadores
(elected by secret vote by the TJ), 2 juízes de direito (elected by
secret vote by the TJ), 1 juiz do TRF, 2 advogados chosen by the
President from a list of 6 prepared by the TJ.

**Tenure** (`CF.121`): same as TSE — 2-year term, max two consecutive
terms, *inamovibilidade*.

**Internal positions**: president and vice-president elected by the
TRE among the desembargadores; the third desembargador serves as
corregedor regional eleitoral. Each TRE adopts its own *regimento
interno*.

**Competência** (CE Art. 29): register parties and candidates; try
electoral crimes committed by juízes eleitorais; hear appeals of
habeas corpus and mandado de segurança in electoral matters; hear
appeals from juízes eleitorais and juntas eleitorais.

**Quorum and voting** (CE Art. 28): majority of members; decisions by
majority vote.

## Juízes eleitorais

Juízes de direito of the Justiça Comum who exercise electoral
jurisdiction temporarily in their comarcas. They are *not* career
electoral judges — they serve alongside their regular caseload.

**Competência** (CE Art. 35): try electoral crimes in the zona; grant
habeas corpus and mandado de segurança in electoral matters; direct
the electoral process in the zona (alistamento, voting, apuração);
divide the zona into seções eleitorais; appoint members of mesas
receptoras.

## Zonas eleitorais — structure and historical evolution

The zona eleitoral is the basic territorial unit of electoral
jurisdiction. Each zona is assigned to a juiz eleitoral. The mapping
between zonas and comarcas is the critical structural fact for
research design: until 1993, every installed comarca automatically
received a corresponding zona eleitoral.

### Creation and early structure (1932–1945)

- **1932**: Decreto 21.076/1932 created the Justiça Eleitoral with
  three tiers: TSE, TREs, and juízes eleitorais in comarcas. TREs
  divided each state into zones mirroring the existing judicial
  divisions (Código Eleitoral Art. 24).
- **1932 division**: most states set zones = comarcas. SP was the
  exception: initially divided into 266 zones (one per município),
  later consolidated to 136 zones with vitalício judges + 129
  supplementary zones.
- **1937–1945**: the Estado Novo constitution (1937) abolished the
  Justiça Eleitoral. DL 7.586/1945 recreated it; Res. TSE 7/1945
  re-established the rule that each state be divided into as many
  zones as comarcas.

### Post-war consolidation (1945–1988)

- **1945–1963**: TSE jurisprudence uniformly held that installation of
  a comarca triggered automatic creation of the corresponding zona
  (Res. TSE 5426/1957). Rare exceptions for municipalities with
  minimal electorate (Res. TSE 3.270/1949, GO).
- **1965 Código Eleitoral** (Lei 4.737/1965): TREs divide the
  circunscrição into zones; TSE approves and homologates.
- **1964–1980**: same rule persisted under military rule — comarca
  installation → zone creation regardless of electorate size (Res.
  TSE 10.386/1978).

### Redemocratization and the freeze (1988–present)

Between 1988 and 2000, Brazil created ~1,438 new municípios. Most had
fewer than 20,000 inhabitants and did not become comarcas, so they
were absorbed into existing zones. This period produced two
load-bearing regulatory changes:

1. **Res. TSE 19.104/1993**: introduced minimum thresholds for zone
   creation — **20,000 electors** in the new zone, with 20,000
   remaining in the zone being split. Broke the automatic
   comarca→zona linkage. Prohibited division-changes in even years
   (Res. 17.895/1992).

2. **Res. TSE 19.197/1997** (and Res. 19.994/1997): nearly tripled
   the threshold to **50,000 electors**, with 50,000 remaining in
   the origin zone. Added geographic and infrastructure criteria.
   Exception for *localidades de difícil acesso* in the North,
   Maranhão, and Mato Grosso (Res. 20.401/1997). Introduced
   *remanejamento* (readjustment of municipality-to-zone assignments
   without creating new zones).

**Effect**: zone creation nearly stopped. From 2,836 zones in 1996 to
2,843 in 1998 and 2,882 in 2002 — near-zero growth despite massive
municipal proliferation. Pet. 387/2003 formalized the freeze: TREs
may reassign municipalities between existing zones but may not create
new ones.

### Research-design implications

- **Zone boundaries are quasi-fixed since 1997.** The 50,000-elector
  threshold effectively froze the map. Zone-level panel data is
  feasible from the late 1990s onward without boundary-change
  contamination.
- **Pre-1993 zone creation is endogenous to comarca installation**,
  which is itself endogenous to state-level judicial reorganization.
  Using zone counts as a measure of state capacity requires
  controlling for the comarca-creation process.
- **Zone ≠ município.** A single zona may span multiple municípios
  (especially small ones absorbed after emancipation). A large
  município may contain multiple zonas (e.g., state capitals). The
  TSE zona-to-município mapping is the linking table.
- **SP is structurally distinct.** In 1932, SP divided by município
  rather than comarca. Post-consolidation, SP zones remain more
  numerous and smaller on average. TRE-SP Res. 614/2023 and Res.
  413/2017 document recent restructuring.
